FOUNDATIONS IN FAITH: CHURCH AND CHRISTOLOGY. Sunday, February 21, 1:30-5:30pm at St. Peter’s Parish Hall. This four hour class focuses two hours on the Church and two hours on Christology. In the Church class, participants will look at the primary images of Church from the Vatican II. They will explore the different roles of the laity and ordained in accomplishing the Church’s mission. In the Christology class, participants will research the divinity and the humanity of Jesus as presented in each of the four Gospels and then defined in the Church Councils. They will explore various titles and images of Jesus present in Catholic spirituality and devotions throughout the centuries to the present time. Please bring your Bible and copy of the Catechism to the class. Cost: $5 per class. (Cosponsored by St. James Cathedral with the South Seattle Deanery) Information, Helen Oesterle, 206-382-4514.

The season of Lent begins with Ash Wednesday. During this time, Catholic Relief Services invites us to journey in solidarity with our global family through Operation Rice Bowl. Donations collected from your Rice Bowl fund development projects that help communities around the world break free of hunger.

What comes out of the Rice Bowl is as important as what goes into it. In each folded-up cardboard bowl is a calendar with a prayer, reflection, fact, or action for every day of Lent, all based on Catholic Social Teaching. Each week the calendar invites you to fast in solidarity with the poor around the world, using a simple meal from the "developing" world. This year, we will learn about life in Lesotho, Bolivia, Afghanistan, Nicaragua, and Ethiopia.

What goes into the bowl is your help to people around the world who face hunger and rising food prices. What comes out of the bowl is a way to walk through Lent in deeper solidarity with the whole human family... It is a journey of prayer, fasting, learning and giving, all with an international flavor.

Be sure to pick up your Rice Bowl on Ash Wednesday!

February 14, 2010

Dear Friends,

As we prepare to embark on our Lenten journey, I can’t help but be aware of something happening this Lent of 2010 that is quite unique. Thanks to your generosity, the archdiocese has purchased a series of television commercials called “Catholics Come Home” which will be appearing on various TV channels throughout the Lenten season. The messages are mainly directed toward Catholics who, for whatever reason, have not been active in the practice of their faith, and they carry an invitation to them to consider coming back home.

I see this as a fine opportunity for all of us to do what we already do very well: to reach out in a warm and welcoming way whenever we see a face we haven’t seen before. And “Catholics Come Home” also provides us with the perfect entrée for personally inviting a friend, neighbor, or family member who has been away from the Church to join us for Mass.

And I want to observe that “coming home” is not just for others! There is really not one of us who does not, in one way or another, need to “come home.” That’s true even if we’ve been coming here regularly for a long time. I say this because “coming home” is not a moment or even a decision: it’s a journey – a personal journey, and a journey made in the company of others. At its deepest and its best, it’s a journey deeper and deeper into the heart of God. And who of us doesn’t need to make that journey?!

So, dear friends, we will all do well to think of this great season of Lent as our “coming home” time. And the Church offers us some wonderful, time-tried ways of doing this. Let me mention the most important ones.

PRAYER. Spending time with God. Giving God time to speak to us, and entering into a heart-to-heart conversation with God. The most important prayer we have is, of course, the Sunday Mass we celebrate together. If we were to do nothing more this Lent than to bring a greater awareness and a greater fervor to each of these Sunday celebrations, we would be accomplishing a great deal. The Sundays of Lent are definitely the right place to start. But we needn’t stop there. Lenten weekday Masses are also a fine way to “come home,” to come closer to Christ -- as many of you know so well. And here are a few other possibilities: taking some time each day to read and reflect prayerfully on a gospel passage; attending Vespers and Benediction of the Blessed Sacrament on Sunday afternoons; joining the Friday evening Taizé ecumenical Prayer around the Cross; praying the Stations of the Cross on Mondays, Wednesdays, or Fridays; or joining in a Lenten Holy Hour on a Friday afternoon.

Still one other great opportunity for prayer and for helping a person to “come home” would be to “adopt” one of our 14 “Elect” (the people who will be baptized at the Easter Vigil). Adopting means taking it on yourself to pray for that person in a special way all during Lent. Prayer cards will be available in the cathedral and in the parish hall during the first few weeks of Lent, each one with the name of one of our Elect. If you take a card, (and I really hope you do), in addition to your prayer, you will be invited when it gets close to Easter to write a card or a short note telling your “adopted” friend of your prayerful support. It strikes me that this is something EVERYONE can do!

FASTING. This, too, can be a personal “coming home,” a coming home to ourselves – perhaps coming to terms with our excesses and with all the ways we pamper ourselves. Fasting can also help us come closer to Jesus who willingly accepted suffering and even death in order to show us the depth of his love for us. What better way to draw close to Jesus than by freely doing what he did: denying ourselves some of life’s comforts? And what better way to draw close to the suffering people of the world—the hungry, the homeless, the hopeless—than by tasting, even in a small way, their cup of suffering?

ALMSGIVING. I like to connect this with fasting. Fasting is best, I think, when it takes us out of ourselves and awakens us to the sufferings of others. For millions upon millions in our world there is no “coming home” because they have no home. Literally! And they have no food or clothing or medicine or any of the other necessities of life. Again this year I commend to you Catholic Relief Services’ Rice Bowl Program as a great vehicle for turning fasting into a work of Christian love. Think what will happen this Lent if you make the Rice Bowl your channel for Lenten almsgiving – putting aside the money you save by eating a little more simply. At the beautiful Mass of Holy Thursday when we invite the parishioners to bring forward their Rice Bowls at the collection time of the Mass, you will see in a powerful way how people who take Lent seriously can make a difference not only in their lives but in the lives of hungry people in our world.

Dear friends, by all means let us warmly welcome all those who have accepted the invitation to “come home” to their Church, but let us also use the opportunity of these holy 40 days to make our own journey home in ways that will bring us closer to God and to one other!


Father Michael G. Ryan
Cathedral Pastor
